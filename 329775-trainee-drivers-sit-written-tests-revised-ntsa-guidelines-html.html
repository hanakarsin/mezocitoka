Trainee Drivers to Sit for Written Tests in Revised NTSA Guidelines

 Posted on April 7, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  526 words  |  Enda Jantzen
  • The trainees will undertake both theoretical and practical tests
  • Continuous assessment tests will be set after every 40 hours of teaching with the final exam coming at the end of the entire training
  • A leaner will be required to meet a pass mark of 80% after compiling the average performance in both theoretical and practical units
  • A trainee who fails a theory exam will have to retake the entire test while one who fails the practical test will only resit for that specific test

Aspiring drivers will now be compelled to sit for written tests before qualifying to get driving licences from any training school, a new guideline by the National Transport Safety Authority (NTSA) has directed.

The trainee drivers will also be required to undertake an elaborate practical exam complete with periodic continuous assessment tests before being considered for graduation.

The new syllabus by NTSA further stipulates the upcoming drivers will be subjected to an exam after every 40 hours of training with a final test set at the end of all the units in a module, Daily Nation reported.

"The theoretical test shall consist of a continuous assessment test administered after every 40 hours. The test shall comprise a minimum of 50 multiple-choice questions,” read the NTSA guidelines," read the guidelines.

For a learner to be considered successful, he or she will be required to attain a pass mark of 80% after compiling the average performance in both theoretical and practical units.

"A performance report is prepared for each learner driver. He or she must demonstrate skills in both theory and practical lessons by attaining a pass mark of 80%," said NTSA.

Unlike in the current mode where new drivers are tried on the theory part on a model town roads board, under the new guidelines, the driver will be tested in a training yard to assess his ability to manoeuvre under different set conditions.

The trainee driver will then be taken to the road to test his ability to practically negotiate most traffic situations under the guidance of a trainer.

Those who will fail the yard training will be compelled to retake the test until they pass before they are allowed to take the road challenge.

A trainee who fails a theory exam will have to retake the entire examination while one who fails the practical test will only resit for that specific exam within a period not exceeding six months.

The training for Public Service Vehicles (PSV) will be more rigorous as the learners will be required to master more complex techniques including vehicle construction and controls, self-inspection of vehicles, communication on the road, speed management, and emergency manoeuvres among other skills.
