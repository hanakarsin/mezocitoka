Eric Donaldson is a famous Jamaican Reggae artist and songwriter. He started writing reggae music at a young age but officially began his career in 1964. As he advanced in his career, he formed a musical group called ‘The West Indians’ whose other members were Wesley Burke and Hector Brooks. The group later changed its name to ‘Kilowatts’ but has since become inactive. Pursuing a solo career was good for the musician as Eric Donaldson songs began getting more airplay.

Eric won the Jamaican Festival Song Competition in the year 1971, 1977, 1978, 1984, 1993 and 1997. His discography is impressive as the musician has released over 15 albums and added 13 compilation albums to his name. Eric Donaldson loves singing about love. A lot of his songs share the message of love whether in relationships, family or society as a whole. Bands like UB40 and The Rolling Stones have done covers for his songs like ‘Cherry Oh Baby’. The following are 15 best Eric Donaldson songs.

1. You Got To Let Me Go

Reggae heads rate this as one of the best roots reggae classics today. The song is off his album ‘Traffic Jam’. Eric Donaldson sings about wanting to be free and going after other things in life. The 71-year-old singer and songwriter sings in moderate tempo as he narrates how he has been controlled before. 'You Get To Let Me Go Now' is a good jam for people who feel trapped in relationships or situations where they feel like they need more space.

2. Land Of My Birth

The reggae beats in this song will automatically make everyone who listens to this tune love Jamaica. The song is from the album ‘Kent Village’ and is a tribute to Eric Donaldson’s birth country; Jamaica. The reggae legend sings about his beautiful country and appreciates the people of Jamaica. He mentions natural resources and everything else the country has. Eric Donaldson’s song could make any tourist want to visit the Caribbean nation.

3. Cherry Oh Baby

This was one of the biggest tunes by Eric Donaldson during the 1980s and 1990s. This timeless classic has the artiste sing praises to his lover. He sings that he misses his lover and wants her to come over and bring him joy. ‘Cherry Oh Baby’ is one of those reggae tracks that make being in love feel beautiful. Eric Donaldson is obviously in love and wishes the person he loves could notice how much he wants her. The song was released in 1971 and is from the album ‘Mr. Pirate’.

4. More Love

Like the title suggests, the song is about love that should come in abundance. In his usual sweet voice, Eric Donaldson sang to encourage human beings to love and appreciate one another. He uses metaphors to pass the message of love across. ‘More Love’ can pass as a Lover’s Rock jam because of the sweet rhythm it has and its melodic nature. Eric Donaldson also shares a few nuggets of wisdom at the end of the song. This track was featured in the album ‘Eric Donaldson Sings 20 Jamaica Classics’.

5. Miserable Woman

The track was released in 1971 and is off his eponymous album. One can detect the tone in the artist's voice as he sings about being taken for a fool by a woman. The listener can feel Eric Donaldson’s pain when he sings about being used as a toy. It is a good track regardless of the heartrending mood it brings.

6. Lonely Nights

This banger has been playing in clubs, radios and TV stations and every other public place since its release in 1982. The track is off the album ‘Come Away’. 7. Cinderella

11. Love Of The Common People

Released in 1971, the track tells the story of poverty and the circumstances poor people navigate through as they survive. Eric Donaldson gives the example of a girl who needs a new dress because the one she owns has patches. In another line, he gives a disturbing example saying that it's a good thing you don't have bus fare because it will fall through the holes in your pocket. The lyrics in this track go on to show how poverty robs people off their dignity. In the end, he sings that the love the family shares is all that matters.

16. What A Festival

Eric was really busy making great music! He won seven consecutive contests breaking the record. This made him acquire the name “Mr Festival”. Produced by the legendary Lloyd Chalmers, What A Festival is a 1993 remix riddim of the favourite Cherry Oh Baby. Its beats and instrumentals are as sweet as those of the original track. The producer did a really excellent and outstanding job. What a Festival was primarily influenced by the seven consecutive awards Eric got as from 1971 in Jamaican annual music festival.

18. Bread of Sorrow

Donaldson uses symbolism in this track to talk about how people in the current world are being misled. In the number, he talks about a vampire that goes above into the light while scorning. Unfortunately, it eats down the bread of sorrows. The vampire thinks it is enjoying, but that turns out to be wrong. Bread Of Sorrow represents the earthly sinful things most people do. The vampire stands for people. Eric prays for Jah to bless everyone in the morning and open their eyes to stop smiling when sinning against Him.
