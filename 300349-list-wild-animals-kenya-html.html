List of wild animals in Kenya

 Posted on March 16, 2024  |  13 minutes  |  2683 words  |  Enda Jantzen

Kenya is one of the top countries with a rich abundance of wild animals, and most of these are found in the country’s national parks. From the world renown Big Five and beautiful birds with dazzling colours to the Ugly Five and the Northern Five, this endless wildlife parade gives tourist eye-popping sightings every day. It would be true to note that these animals are too numerous to be mentioned one by one. Still, lions are the most sought after attraction in the numerous African wildlife safaris across the country.

So, what animals can be found in Kenya?

The Big 5

This term refers to the five African wild animals that the early big-game hunters perceived to be the most dangerous and difficult to hunt for food. Kenya is fortunate to host all the animals in this category.

African elephant

This gigantic animal is a memorable sight and is among the most iconic species within the east African region. On average, an adult elephant weighs six tons and the majestic creature stands tall and strong up to feet.

Notably, the massive mammal can be found in different areas of the country, but it is the famous Shimba Hills Reserve in particular that is well-known as home to the largest elephant population. They are important to the ecosystem in that as they bring down trees as they try to reach softer leaves at the top. This way, they create more space for savanna vegetation that grazers can enjoy. However, they are also known to wreak havoc in farms in different parts of the country.

While they may be protected, the species' survival faces significant threat due to consistent poaching for tusks.

Lion

What is the national animal of Kenya? Ask any Kenyan this and they will tell you how symbolic the animal is. With his imposing roar and luxurious mane, the male lion is perceived as the king of the jungle. On average, an adult weighs up to 420 pounds. The female counterpart, however, has a lesser mass.

A pride of lions is usually symbolic of the continent’s wildlife, and as aforementioned, they are the most popular with safari travelers. As opposed to other carts that prefer solitary lifestyles, lions exhibit a different social behavior. A single male is tasked with ruling a group, and the females take part in hunting. They prey on large grazing animals such as zebras, gazelles, buffalo, or wildebeest.

The best location to view lions is the Masai Mara National Reserve.

Leopard

This big cat is also part of the big five and it’s known for dragging prey onto the savanna scrub trees to avoid scavengers. For prey, this fantastic predator will consume whatever it can hunt and kill, and this includes much larger animals than those eaten by lions. Their population is relatively large in the country and they are not among the endangered species of the country.

The all-black leopard variety (also known as the black panther) is also available and has been spotted in different areas in the country, Laikipia sighting being the most recent.

Rhinoceros

Kenya is home to two species of the rhino; the white and black variables (even though both of them are gray in color). Across the world, the black rhino is one of the critically endangered species, and the number of white rhinos in the country is only a handful. Regardless, the white variety exists in higher numbers as you head further south of the continent.

Even though they are among the most protected species, the magnificent herbivores are frequently hunted for their horns.

Cape buffalo

This is the largest of the continent’s buffaloes and it weighs in at approximately half a ton. The massive creatures reside in numerous savanna landscapes in the country and feed on large grass quantities.

The Northern 5

The iconic wild animals got the name due to the fact that they primarily reside in conservancies and national park towards the north of the capital, Nairobi, in a region of over 65,000 square kilometers. Here are some of the animals in this category.

Gerenuk/giraffe gazelle

The animal got its name from a Somali phrase that can be translated to ‘giraffe-necked’. Also referred to as the Waller’s Gazelle, this creature resembles an ordinary gazelle. However, you can distinguish it with its long pencil-like neck. This is the rarest of the Northern Five and it takes advantage of the long legs and neck by reaching for food in shrubs and trees.

These herbivores feed on flowers, fruit, and leaves by yanking branches toward them as opposed to eating closer to the ground. They are relatively shy compared to their counterparts in the antelope family, and live in small hears composed of at eight animals at most.

East African Oryx/beisa

Resembling a cross between a gazelle and a goat, the Baisa can be easily spotted due to their large horns that look like antlers. Their color ranges from white, black to brown, and stay in the semi-desert and savanna climates that characterizes their northern habitat. Just like camels, the animals have adapted genetically and can preserve a lot of water. Also, they cannot sweat at temperatures below 116 degrees Fahrenheit.

Another notable behaviour of the species is that there’s usually an alpha bull or head cow that is usually in charge of the herd.

Reticulated giraffe

With their noteworthy long neck, this giraffe species is also among the common sightings in the north of the equator. These animals use their necks to obtain fruit and leaves in the top acacia trees, and you can identify a tree that has just been feasted on. This is because the animal tends to eat from the top where they can find the freshest leaves, leaving the trees looking a bit like hats.

They’re hardly ever seen drinking water, and look quite funny when they do. Their long necks combined with powerful eyes make for good eyesight.

Each of these animals is unique and you can distinguish them by the pattern of spots in their bodies that slightly differ. The darker the spots, the older the animal.

Grevy’s zebra

With the easy to identify stripes, these are among the country’s beloved and well-known animals. The Grevy’s Zebra, on the other hand, is the largest in the species and has several features that make it different from the normal zebra. For instance, it has more stripes than the common ones and also has larger ears. In total, they have 80 stripes and have a stronger sense of sight.

Interestingly, its moniker makes the animal even more unique: It got the name in 1882, named after the then French president, Jules Grevy. He was gifted with one of the special zebras. But unfortunately, it continues to be endangered due to draught and habitat loss. Currently, there are only 2,700 of them in the world and a 11 percent of these are housed in the Lewa Conservancy.

Ostrich

Ostriches are large flightless birds that live in small groups on the plains of Kenya. They lay their eggs in "nests" scratched into the bare earth. Ostriches eat mainly plant material, along with insects. Their long legs can give a powerful kick to ward of predators, and they can run at a steady speed of 30 mph.

The Ugly 5

As the title suggests, the animals come with their own ‘unique’ appearances. Regardless, they are among the animals that attract a lot of tourists. Before beginning to wonder why someone would pay to see an ugly animal, here is a list of wildlife in this category.

Warthog

This magnificent animal is a member of the pig family and you can easily recognize it with the ‘warts’ and tusks on the head that are used to dig burrows and extract edible roots from the ground. These are among the most common animals in the country, and they spread in different regions of Kenya. Notably, it is not among the endangered species in Kenya.

Marabou stork

With the black wings, hunched stature, pink neck pouch, this is definitely not among the prettiest birds in the country. However, they serve a crucial purpose in the ecosystems by scavenging of foods like carcasses, smaller birds, termites, and many more.

The notable neck pouch is a crucial feature and the bird uses it to produce some distinctive croaking sound mostly heard in the mating season. This bird is also distinguishable for its white tail features that are quite long and a featherless head.

White-backed vulture

The bird got its name from the white-feathered back, but the rest of the body is covered with gray. Just like other members of the vulture family, the bird scavenges on animal carcasses and does not have natural predators. You will notice them circling around plain areas and open savanna to identify potential food.

After sighting blood from a kill, it will sound an alarm to notify other vultures and soon they will be on the ground alongside others such as jackals, hyenas etc. The iconic bird doesn’t fear snatching food from lionesses or cheetahs, but they will never get on the way of the ‘king of the jungle.

They may not have predators, but the birds are still endangered due to habitat loss and irresponsible hunting.

Spotted hyena

Due to their caricature depiction in the movie ‘The Lion King’, these animals are usually associated with scheming ways and an evil crackle. But there’s more to the misunderstood species than meets the eye. These creatures are remarkably strong, have keen hunting skills and powerful jaws. They prey on various animals in the Kenyan wilderness such as zebras, antelopes or even buffaloes.

Moreover, they are fast hunters and can clock speeds of up to 37 miles per hour in short bursts to catch prey. They live as a complex matriarchal society in clans and they use certain sounds to unite the members together in case of danger or when there is a hunt afoot. The small pups (six-month-olds and below) are not part of the hunt and they remain under the watch of some adult females.

Wildebeest

The grazing wildebeest is among the most popular wildlife in the country. This has been facilitated by the annual Mara migration where the animals, alongside different other grazers, migrate in search of pastures leading to a natural spectacle. Also known as ‘gnu’, the wildebeest is related to both cows and antelopes.

Exotic wildlife species in Kenya

Kenya is home to lots of forests, savanna, national parks and reserves, and all these ecosystems serve as homes to plenty of plant and animal species. Notably, a good number of these organisms aren’t indigenous in the country, but have grown to become part of the country’s wildlife heritage. Here are some good examples.

The Sitatunga

Also referred to as the marshbuck, the antelope species is mainly found in swampy areas. It is believed that the amazing herbivore originated from central African countries like Cameroon and the DRC. What distinguishes the antelopes from most others in the same family is their semi-aquatic nature and the fact that they are comfortable in water.

You can find them in Saiwa Swamp National Park, a few miles from the agricultural town of Kitale. Due to the residents, the park has gained popularity as ‘Home of the Sitatunga’.

Nile Crocodile

The iconic creature is said to be the continent’s largest crocodilian and its origins are traced to the Nile River basin. Currently, they live in many rivers as well as national parks. While the crocodiles may be part of an interesting reptile species, these carnivores are vicious and can feed on anything from wildebeests to zebras and even humans.

Vervet Monkey

This is among the more popular species in Africa and is more popular in countries towards the south of the continent. The variety has a black face with grey fur, with other parts of the body being white. They are common in a majority of national parks in the country, but a good number is in parks such as Shimba Hills, Ol Donyo Sabuk, and Lake Nakuru. Also, they can be found in the coastal areas.

Aardwolf

This mammal feeds on insects and seek shelter by burrowing underground. It is believed that they originated from the South African countries and this explains the origin of the name, but you can find some subspecies in different parts of the country. Notably, the animal is related to stripped hyenas and is also among the most sought after wildlife.

Striped Hyenas

This hyena species is different from other spotted hyenas due to the dark stripes on their grey and brown body fur. It also has several distinct facial features that are bigger than their small body size. They reside in dry, rocky or bushy areas. The subspecies available in the country is the Sudanese stripped Hyena and you can find it in areas like the Nairobi Animal Orphanage and Sibiloi National Park.

Duiker

This antelope variety lives either in the bushes or forest and has several sub species residing in the ocunrty. They include the Red Duiker, Bush Duiker, and Blue Duiker that are all found in the renown Shimba Hills Natinal Reserve. Kenya is also home to the Ader’s Duiker.

The Burmese Star Tortoise

This species traces its origins in the forests in Burma and is believed to be nearing extinction. Kenya is among the few African countries that are fortunate to host the creature.

Ring-necked parakeet

The ring-necked parakeet is a bird species that, like the name says, has a black or red ring on the neck. It is also referred to as rose-ringed parakeet and it has been spotted in Kenya, although the main subspecies trace back to other African countries and Asia.

As the name suggests, this bird stands out with the red or black ring on the neck and are also known as rose-ringed parakeet. The origin can be traced to other countries in the continent as well as Asia.

House Crow

The House Crow is an exotic bird of Asian native that can be found in Kenya. It has black and grey fur and it is said to be omnivorous. Through flying sometimes between ships, it has reached other parts of the world.
